Salt

Bad Reichenhall is a significant district town in the administrative area of Upper Bavaria and the district town of Berchtesgadener Land. The city has a four thousand year history that is intimately related to the mining of salt from brine. In Germany, Bad Reichenhaller Alpensalz has a market share of more than 50%. Since the middle of the nineteenth century, the town has been recognized as a spa and holiday destination; the spa has been in its name since 1890, and the town was accorded the title of Royal Bavarian State Spa in 1899. The spa town is located in Saalachtal, which is surrounded by the slopes of the Berchtesgaden and Chiemgau Alps.

During the Bell Beaker civilization, there was already a permanent settlement and significant trading networks, according to finds in the Reichenhall area (2600 to 2300 BC). This was only accurately verified by the discovery of several burial sites from the Urnfield period (1600-750 BC) in the vicinity of today's Karlstein district. There was also a Celtic worship site in the Langacker valley and a mint at the "Haiderburgstein" during the La Tène period (450-15 BC).

Stone

A stone is a fragment of rock. It is a hard, compressed mineral mass. The term is frequently used to refer to a little chunk of rock. Natural rock, particularly as a construction material, is frequently referred to as "stone." Granite, marble, and sandstone are examples of natural stones used in construction. Stone does not include manufactured, artificial items such as concrete or clay bricks. The stone takes time to heat up and stays hot for a long time. It has poor electrical conductivity. Stone was among the earliest materials used to create tools and structures. It's a really strong substance. It is less susceptible to weather than wood or brick. Stone weathers significantly more slowly depending on the type of rock.

Potato

The Spanish brought potatoes from the Americas to Europe in the second part of the 16th century. They are now a staple food in many regions of the world, as well as an essential component of much of the world's food supply. Potatoes were the world's fourth-largest food crop in 2014, after only maize (corn), wheat, and rice. There are currently over 5,000 different varieties of potatoes as a result of millennia of selective breeding. Over 99% of the potatoes grown now are descended from cultivars that originated in the plains of south-central Chile. The potato's value as a food source and culinary element varies by area and is always changing. It is still an important crop in Europe, particularly in Northern and Eastern Europe.

Pre-Columbian farmers initially cultivated the potato near Lake Titicaca in modern-day southern Peru and northwestern Bolivia. Since then, it has expanded around the world and has become a staple crop in many nations. The oldest archaeologically proven potato tuber remnants date from 2500 BC and were discovered at the coastal settlement of Ancon (central Peru). Solanum tuberosum tuberosum, the most extensively grown variety, is endemic to the Chiloé Archipelago and has been farmed by the local indigenous people since before the Spanish invasion.

Soap

It is unknown who was the first to develop soap. The oldest indication of soap-like compounds being produced goes back to roughly 2800 BC in ancient Babylon. Around 2500 BC, a method for making soap was written on a Sumerian clay tablet; the soap was made by heating a mixture of oil and wood ash, the first documented chemical reaction, and was used to wash woolen clothes. According to the Ebers papyrus (Egypt, 1550 BC), the ancient Egyptians used soap as a remedy and made their soaps by combining animal fats or vegetable oils with a soda ash material called Trona. According to Egyptian texts, a similar chemical was employed in the processing of wool for weaving.

In the late sixth century (when Naples was under the rule of the Eastern Roman Empire), soapmakers formed a guild, and by the eighth century, soap-making was well known in Italy and Spain. The Carolingian capitulary De Villis, which dates approximately 800 and represents Charlemagne's royal testament, includes soap as one of the items the stewards of royal estates are to tally. By 800, Medieval Spain's territories were a dominant soapmaker, while soapmaking began in the Kingdom of England about 1200. Soapmaking is cited as both "women's labour" and "excellent workmen's output," among other requirements such as carpenters', blacksmiths', and bakers' produce.

Milk

In the late 1990s, milk bags were used in Australia (Greater Shepparton, Victoria), and were supplied by the Shepparton-based dairy firm Ducats. At the 1970s and early 1980s, they were also used in Gympie, Queensland, and about the same period in Caboolture, Queensland. These were a pint size.

In the United Kingdom, Sainsbury's conducted a test operation with Dairy Crest in 2008 to distribute milk in bags. It was initially intended for 35 retailers at the same price as a conventional 2-imperial-pint (1.1 l) plastic milk bottle. In 2010, the product was expanded nationwide, and the bags were sold at a lower price than standard containers. The bags are typically used in conjunction with a particular plastic jug in the United Kingdom. The bag fits snuggly inside the jug; one corner of the bag is locked under a bar at the front of the jug, and when the top is closed, the bag is perforated and a spout glides into the hole, enabling the milk to be readily dispensed while keeping freshness.

Shampoo

Since ancient times, a variety of plants and their extracts have been utilized as shampoo on the Indian subcontinent. Shampoo originated in the Indus Valley Civilization. Sapindus was boiled with dried Indian gooseberry (amla) and a variety of other herbs, and the filtered extract was used to make a highly efficient early shampoo. Sapindus, often known as soapberries or soapnuts, is a tropical tree found across India. Its fruit pulp contains saponins, which function as a natural surfactant. Soapberry extract produces a lather known as phenaka in Indian scriptures. It makes the hair smooth, lustrous(reflecting light evenly), and easy to handle. Shikakai (Acacia concinna), hibiscus blossoms, and ritha were also used to cleanse the hair.

Sake Dean Mahomed, a Bengali wanderer, surgeon, and entrepreneur, is credited for bringing shampooing to the United Kingdom. Mahomed created the first commercial "shampooing" vapor masseur bath in England, in Brighton, in 1814, with his Irish wife Jane Daly. In a local newspaper, he defined the therapy as "the Indian Medicated Vapor Bath (kind of Turkish bath), a remedy to many ailments and offering full relief when everything else fails; notably Rheumatic and paralytic, gout, stiff joints, old sprains, lame legs, aches and pains in the joints."

Gas Mask

To allow vision, the original gas masks usually featured round lenses composed of glass, mica, or cellulose acetate. Glass and mica were extremely fragile and required to be replaced on a regular basis. The subsequent Triplex lens design (a cellulose acetate lens sandwiched between two glass lenses) grew increasingly popular, and it was the standard until the 1930s, alongside plain cellulose acetate. Panoramic lenses did not become popular until the 1930s, however there are cases of them being used even during WWII . Later, stronger polycarbonate was used.

According to Popular Mechanics, "the ordinary sponge was employed as a gas mask in ancient Greece." Jean-François Pilâtre de Rozier created the respirator in 1785. Miners utilized primitive respirator prototypes, which Alexander von Humboldt presented in 1799 while working as a mining engineer in Prussia. Lewis P. Haslett devised the predecessor of the current gas mask in 1847, a device that had features that permitted breathing through a nose and mouthpiece, intake of air through a bulb-shaped filter, and exhalation of air back into the atmosphere. According to First Facts, Lewis Phectic patented a "gas mask like the present variety."
